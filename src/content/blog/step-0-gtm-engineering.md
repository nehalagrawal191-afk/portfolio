There's a version of this post that opens with a tools list. Clay for enrichment, Apollo for sourcing, n8n for automation, Lemlist for sequences. I've written that version. I've also watched it age badly every six months when something new comes out and the stack reshuffles.

What I've noticed — both from building GTM infrastructure at Invigilo and from talking to founders doing the same thing — is that most teams start with tools and work backwards. They see someone on LinkedIn talking about their $300 setup replacing $300k teams, copy it, and then spend the next few months wondering why the outputs don't match the case study.

My hunch is the stack was never the variable. The foundation underneath it was.

I've said it before, I'll say it again: GTM engineering is one piece of this. It's not the whole thing.

After a lot of iteration — and honestly, a lot of wasted hours — here's how I actually think about it now when I'm working through this for Invigilo or talking to other early-stage teams.

**The stack is the last decision you make. Everything before it is clarity work.**

## 1. Start with the customer, not the product

- Start with a real buyer (someone who already bought or would within 30 days)
- Define industry + company stage clearly (not broad categories)
- Specify exact role + seniority (who actually owns the problem)
- Identify the active problem they are trying to solve right now
- Confirm urgency / trigger event (why now, not later)
- Check for buying signals (recent tools, hiring, funding, behavior)
- Exclude close-but-wrong profiles (who looks similar but won't convert)
- Validate scale (can you find 50–100 real matches today?)

## 2. Map what revenue actually looks like

What does a closed deal look like? What size, what sales cycle, what does the buyer need to see before they move? This shapes everything downstream — how you prioritise channels, what counts as a qualified lead, what "working" means for your outreach.

## 3. Define the pipeline stages that reflect your actual process

Not the default CRM stages. The real ones. Where do leads come from, what moves them forward, where do they stall, and what information do you need at each stage to make a good decision. If you can't draw this on a whiteboard, the tooling will automate confusion.

## 4. Mine what already worked

Past customers, closed deals, even deals that got to late stage and fell apart — there's more signal there than in any tool. What did those people have in common? How did they find you? What made them move fast? This is the most underused input in GTM strategy.

## 5. Now build the stack

Once you know who you're going after, what the pipeline looks like, and what signals matter — the tooling decisions get a lot simpler. You're not picking tools based on what's popular. You're picking tools based on what specific jobs need to get done. Sourcing, enrichment, outreach, tracking, handoff. Each one has a clear function, and you can evaluate options against that function rather than against vibes.

The teams I've seen get this right aren't necessarily using the most sophisticated stack. They're using a stack that fits a strategy they actually understand. The ones struggling are usually running expensive, well-integrated tooling on top of an ICP they're still figuring out.

**Fix the thinking first. The tools follow.**
